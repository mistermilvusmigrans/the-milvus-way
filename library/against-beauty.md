<h1>Against Beauty</h1>

---

In "Beauty is Downstream of Love" ([library/lifeblood-essays.odt](./lifeblood-essays.odt)), I defined beauty as follows:

> Beauty is a form of evaluation, and in particular, it is the internalised evaluation of an object (be it a person, a place, a thing, an idea, etc.) as embodying/reflecting values and/or virtues. Here, there are 2 aspects that are relevant: (1) internalisation, and (2) moral evaluation.

I explained why I consider moral evaluation as relevant here:

> The second aspect is **moral evaluation**. This aspect differentiates beauty from the broader category of internalised judgement, as it implies that the judgement is specifically relates to our core convictions about the value, rather than a preference-based judgement. It is often said that beauty is subjective (“in the eye of the beholder”), and though it is true that beauty needs the subject as much as the object, the aspect of moral evaluation in beauty differentiates it from other “subjective” judgements such as taste, preference or momentary/situational desire. If we conflate beauty, taste, preference and momentary/situational desire, we lose out the concept that arises from the fact that moral premises have an effect on our psychological context and lived experience, and this is precisely the concept that beauty captures.

Now, consider the range of uses the word "beauty" has:

- A beautiful motorcycle
- A beautiful mathematical proof
- A beautiful story
- A beautiful picture
- A beautiful person
- A beautiful creature

And so on.

Now, consider the fact that "beauty" and the standards for it in any given domain can vary greatly, from person to person, from culture to culture, from era to era. Some may regard a crow as ugly, while others adore its "understated" beauty. Some may regard a person as ugly, while others may appreciate their "unconventional" attractiveness. Beauty standards for men and women varied across place and time, from fuller figures to slender frames, from "well-toned" physiques to "chiseled" looks, from makeup and touch-up as key to beauty (look at news reporters across the world, especially female news reporters, or various female stars/celebrities) to makeup and touch-up as "ungraceful" or "dishonest" (see those who speak of "aging gracefully" or those who appreciate "normal" features and faces). Some may regard their loved ones as beautiful regardless of the opinions of others, with "beauty" serving almost as an expression of adoration or affection rather than any standard-based evaluation.

A real conceptual mess, is it not?

When I think about it, "beauty" often appears on the surface as a shorthand for "I find this attractive/pleasing" or even "I find this adorable/loveable". However, this is not all "beauty" means, because if it were, we could substitute "beautiful" for any of these more specific, personally-motivated descriptions. Rather, "beauty" also often smuggles in evaluation about the worth of something/someone, i.e. about how much consideration and/or appreciation they deserve. Beauty, especially in women, is often shown in media (movies and stories) as deserving of "love" (romantic love in particular) primarily for their beauty. Beauty may make people like you more, care for you more, be more appreciate your presence and your views more, ascribe more positive traits without any evidence for them, assign desirability regardless of moral merit.

Now, beauty on its own may be a harmless concept, vague and often unhelpful in its imprecision and sometimes harmful in its smuggled value-judgements, but perhaps mostly harmless. But I think its imprecision as a concept serves as a tool to prop up restrictive roles and discriminatory value-judgements. For example, consider how "beauty" is often regarded as a woman's primary "asset", and how youth is equated to beauty, tying it as well to fertility and sexual appeal. Now consider how that shapes a woman's self-concept, her view of her own worth, her view of what paths of pursuing value she sees open to her, the way she judges herself and compares herself to others. Consider how this changes with age. Consider the insecurity, the jealousy that comes from it, the excess of investment in appearance, the dearth of investment in life-skills and finances. This is disheartening enough to consider, not even considering how this shapes men's views on women, women's worth and what they look for in potential partners. What about maturity? Thoughtfulness? Wisdom? What about any ingredients for an equal, mutually-beneficial partnership? These are likely to be sidelined for something as fleeting and outside one's control as youth.

Now, is it unfair to look down on the concept of beauty just because it has been used to look down on neutral or even valuable traits? No, I think it is not unfair, because it is real. Moreover, I do not see what value such a vague concept brings when more precise, honest descriptions and even evaluations exist. Is a painting "beautiful"? Or is it vivid, masterful, uplifting, haunting, etc.? Is a person "beautiful"? Or are they striking in appearance, pleasing to watch, well-put-together, etc.? When you remove the word "beauty" and put in its place the descriptions of what you actually observe, experience and/or think about, you remove unwarranted value-judgement, unjustified comparison, unclear connections between how what you experience ties to what you value.

I defined beauty. Now, I want to abandon the concept.

---

Internalised moral evaluation is honest.

Attractiveness/appeal is honest.

"Beauty" is a potential smuggler of who knows what.
