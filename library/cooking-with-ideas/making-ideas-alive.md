# Context
In [living-ideas.md](./living-ideas.md), I have discussed the concept of a living idea. In that article, I said the following: *"I shall define "living" ideas to differentiate between ideas that are reliably concretisable and thus usable in concrete action/decision-making, versus ideas that are inert, i.e. floating abstractions or abstractions that do not generate insights in concrete contexts."* The essay also mentions the ontological position that only particulars exist, and while this position was used to ground the importance of the concept of a living idea, I realise now that this was not necessary. The concept of a living idea is important because it is what pushes the mind - our most power tool for grasping and dealing with reality - to actually bite reality. In the current article, I commit to this notion and attempt to elucidate the approach - broadly defined - of making ideas alive.

# Part 1: Substance
- Thinking rests on integration and differentiation
- Either act requires stuff to integrate or differentiate
- Acquiring this stuff is essential

---

In the article [*AI Agents for Claims Automation: A Complete Guide*, www.v7labs.com/blog/automated-claims-processing-for-insurance](https://www.v7labs.com/blog/automated-claims-processing-for-insurance), data extraction is divided into two components: facts (as gathered by OCR) and context (as gathered by LLMs). Facts are the details, the differentiated specifics. Context is the integration of facts into an environment within which cognition happens - not discrete packets of data but interrelated, interacting components. Both are essential, because the context is made of facts and because facts do not exist in isolation (or if they do, they are not important to the environment we are dealing with). The result of data extraction - facts and context - are what I name "substance", and substance is what ideas are meant to draw from and bite into.

---

Some guidelines regarding substance:

- Gather facts before contextualising them
- Contextualisation must trace back to facts and assumptions
    > Assumptions must be stated as such.
- No idea exists out of context - thus, gather the context first

This can involve:

- Collecting data without trying to shoehorn them into models
- Integrating iteratively, revising and reframing if needed

# Part 2: Effort
- Conceptual thinking is not automatic
- Thus, grasping and concretising ideas takes effort
- This effort is not only unavoidable but necessary

---

In my understanding, effort toward ideas is primarily about directing and sustaining focus. Now, I can say from experience and a broader understanding of the concept of "focus" that directing and sustaining focus does not necessarily mean concentration or unwavering commitment. For example, when reading technical papers for a project, I found the effort very hard to sustain without descending into uncomprehending mental repetition of what I was receiving from the text. Here, directing focus consisted of recognising this fact and taking breaks when I needed. This approach helped me actually focus when I returned, and eventually helped me absorb the whole paper.

The analogy I can make is that of a sail. Focus is that which directs the sail of a ship, not that which propels the ship - at least not on its own. You adjust as per the wind, you plan your path as per weather patterns, and you deal with storms when the arise the best you can. The sail is (usually) in your hands, but what you can do with it depends on a lot factors that you must pay attention to.

---

Effort about ideas themselves can involve:

- Framing received ideas in your own words
- Finding your own examples/instantiations for ideas
- Connecting ideas to other ideas you grasp more clearly
- Evaluating ideas as per the evidence

Effort about the pursuit of comprehension can involve:

- Paying attention to the factors affecting your focus
- Pausing and planning and replanning your study approach
- Allowing focus to wander enough to catch new insights
- Directing focus enough to untangle its implications

# Part 3: Consolidation
- To ideate about interaction, you must make ideas interact
- To ideate about relationships, you must interrelate ideas
- Interaction and relationships are foundational to reality

---

This is similar to contextualisation, but it involves not just integrating facts into a context but integrating ideas within a context, understanding not just the ideas on their but how they generate insights, i.e. new knowledge that further informs the pursuit of your purposes.

---

This can involve:

- Logical structuring/organisation
- Documenting and reporting on findings
- Presenting results

# My way of grasping these parts
The following is not definitive, but is instructive to me:

- Substance => Reading, listening, taking notes
- Effort => White-boarding, writing, exercising
- Consolidation => Documentation, essay writing, presentation

I find that each part feeds into the other:

- Consolidation and effort add substance to study
- Substance can consolidate data into context
- Effort applies throughout

In truth, I find that these parts are not strictly distinct, separate components of cognition, but rather, mor often than not, different ways of looking at cognition. What they offer, often, is not distinct parts of cognition but distinct entry-points into cognition. Cognition cannot exist without any one of them.

# How I consider this article
I have often had the tendency, when it came to essays and articles like this, to come up with a framework that is robust, extensible, and fundamentally complete (lacking only in details and application-level specifics). In this article, I have come up with a framework, and I do believe it holds up in many ways. But it has gaps. What does it mean to gather facts before contextualising them, given that we always have some context already? How to learn to "sail the ship", so to speak, i.e. how to direct and sustain your focus in a given context? While studying? While working? While resting or exploring or engaging with loved ones? And how to make sure consolidation does not result in more ideas that eventually become inert (as has been the case with much of my studies)?

To this, I answer: I cannot lay this out in words. It is a practice, a self-correcting, continually recalibrating, sometimes stumbling, sometimes smooth practice. This whole article was inspired by the messy yet effective way in which I took notes, whiteboarded, documented, read and implemented a project I have been working on for the past few months. I realised each of these activities - taking notes, whiteboarding, documenting, reading, implementing - had a distinct contribution to how well I could understand and use my ideas. This article is an attempt to capture this mess, like a snapshot, knowing, however, that the dance cannot be captured in a snapshot alone. Take this article as a reference point, a checkpoint of sorts. The practice continues.

This is not to say that anything discussed here is "just words". I mean the words I have written here, and I think they offer valuable, grounded insights. However, they are simply not complete. Not false or "up in the air", just not complete. It is like explaining how a sail works. It has valuable, grounded insights, but actual sailing takes a lot more. This is not an incompleteness due to a lack of words, although a lot more of great value can be said for the themes of this article. It is, fundamentally, an incompleteness due to this being the wrong medium for a whole host of other valuable, grounded insights, such as those gained by day-to-day practice, by engaging with real-life projects, by trial-and-error, by gradually-built intuition, etc.
