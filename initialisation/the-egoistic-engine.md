# Naming what was implicit
This morality is entirely egoistic. The starting point is always a particular self, with limited time and capacity, pursuing its own contact with reality. There is no external obligation, no other-regarding principle at the foundation.

# Why egoism does not terminate in selfishness
> Because reality includes me.

If one is genuinely trying to maximise contact with reality, one cannot systematically ignore the reality of other minds - that is an epistemic failure (see: [the-baseline-obligation.md](./the-baseline-obligation.md)). This means concern for others does not need to be grafted on as an external obligation; it *emerges* from the honest pursuit of the egoist project. This is a stronger foundation for other-regarding behaviour than most moral systems provide: I do not care about others because a rule says so; I care because failing to do so means I am not actually in contact with reality.

# The honest cost
The framework can only obligate engagement with the reality of others insofar as that reality is accessible and relevant to me. Does someone whose situation doesn't bring them into contact with certain suffering or injustice have an obligation to seek that contact out? In traditional moral frameworks: straightforwardly yes. In this framework: it must be *derived*, and the derivation is harder. One might argue reality is interconnected enough that no genuine isolation exists, that distant suffering shapes the world one inhabits regardless of whether it is seen. This may be true, but this is an empirical argument, not a foundational one.

# Methodological, not substantive
> - The engine is egoistic
> - The conclusions need not be

The self is where knowing begins, not where knowing ends. A self genuinely in contact with reality will find that reality is full of others, full of consequences that ripple outward, full of truths that exceed and sometimes rebuke the self's initial preferences.
