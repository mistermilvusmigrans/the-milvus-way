# The practical difficulty
The framework risks drifting into disembodied abstraction unless alternated with concrete enactment. Constant vigilance is empirically unsustainable and inefficient - vigilance presupposes I am looking for something, and if I do not know roughly what, I exhaust myself into nothing. Hence, the key question is: ***what orientation should actually be maintained, in a practicable sense, not just a theoretical one?***

# Why general vigilance fails
"Watch for everything that might be wrong" produces diffuse anxiety, not focus, because attention unshaped by some expectation of what matters has no way to allocate itself. This means it treats every input as equally worth scanning - exactly the opposite of the leverage principle established earlier (see: [the-hierarchy-of-knowledge-seeking.md](./the-hierarchy-of-knowledge-seeking.md)). High-leverage focus, not maximal focus, was always the standard.

# The actual orientation: the gap, not the totality
I am not scanning all of reality for distortion. I am watching for the moment of *divergence* - when my action, speech, or reasoning stops extending cleanly from what is, and starts extending from something else: a wish, a fear, a preferred story, a flinch.

# Recognisable signatures of divergence
That divergence has a recognisable feel. It tends to show up as one of a small number of signatures - noticing:

- I am defending a conclusion instead of testing it
- I am avoiding a piece of information rather than seeking it
- A felt urgency to conclude, rather than an urgency to see clearly

# Why this is more practicable
Because it is pattern-recognition, not exhaustive scanning, like training my ear to catch one particular birdcall in a forest full of noise. Attention is tuned to one signature and catches it without strain once trained, rather than listening to every sound.

# Concrete case
Take an argument with someone me love. The abstract instruction - "focus on what is" - is useless mid-sentence, too vague to act on in the moment. But the instruction - "notice the moment my account of what they said stops matching what they actually said" - is catchable in real time: a small, specific, trainable trigger. The pull to summarise their position uncharitably - because it's more satisfying to argue against the uncharitable version - noticed, is the entire practice. Constant vigilance over the whole conversation is not required; a trained reflex for that one specific signature of divergence is.

# The answer
Cultivate a small number of trained signatures of self-deception or narrowed focus, specific to how *I* personally tend to diverge, and let detection of those be light, automatic, low-cost - rather than attempting to hold the entirety of reality in view at all times. This is also why memory of one's own patterns matters so practically: those patterns are not only data for retrospective calibration - they are precisely what the light-touch attention is trained to scan for. Not vigilance in general. Tuning to one's own specific failure modes.
