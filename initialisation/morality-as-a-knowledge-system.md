# Core: *what is morality and what is it for*
- Not morality as a:
  - Set of rules
  - Set of edicts
  - Set of values deemed worthy by some higher authority
- Morality as a:
  - Knowledge-base
  - Model for what does and does not work <br> **... in the pursuit of a single aim**:
    > To be in contact with reality

"To be in contact with reality"

=> To seek truth:

- Not as an abstraction
- As genuine correspondence with what is

# The pursuit of value collapses into the pursuit of truth
This system collapses the distinction between pursuing values and truth, and hence, between how one ought to live and what knowledge one ought to seek. Here, it is key to clarify what "contact with reality" means. It is true that any being is always in some contact with reality just by being. But contact varies greatly in depth and detail. A person who thinks reality should reshape itself as per their own desires is in contact with reality in a thin sense, i.e. they feel, they act. But their model of reality does not correspond with the fact that reality for the most part exists independently of one's desires. Hence, by "contact", I mean something more specific and more elaborate: correspondence between my model of the world and the world as it is, including its substances, its material and causal structures, its other inhabitants, etc.

Hence:

> Morality is what enables/facilitates correspondence between:
> 
> - What one holds in one's mind
> - What is

# Virtue is load-bearing, authority is irrelevant
Considering the above, we see that this premise of morality as a knowledge system makes the virtues load-bearing, not arbitrary. Honesty, temperance, courage to look at uncomfortable truths - these are not handed-down prescriptions but functional requirements. For example, considering honestly, I cannot be in contact with reality while consistently and/or systematically deceiving myself or others; the moral failure and the epistemic failure are the same one failure.

Considering the above, we see that this premise of morality as a knowledge system also dissolves the problem of authority, i.e. the problem of "who sets the standards", because the standard is reality itself, not human-made.

> Reality does not need to justify itself.
