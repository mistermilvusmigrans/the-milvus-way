# Key question
If...

- morality is the pursuit of maximal contact with reality
- our minds and tiI am limited

... then we face practical questions:

- What knowledge to seek, and why?
- In what order, and why?
- What to ignore, and why?
- And what does *maximal* mean here?

# Illustrative example: understanding how bridges work
> **KEY IDEA 1**: Some knowledge covers a wider range of contexts.

Suppose I want to understand how bridges work. I could study the properties of steel, concrete, and timber in exhaustive detail. Or I could begin with physics and mechanics, which is broader, omitting a number of specifics while clarifying the mechanics by which the behaviour of any material can be accounted for and understood. I come to know how bridges work more deeply through the latter, even though I know fewer specific facts.

> **KEY IDEA 2**: Some knowledge is generative of other knowledge.

In my example, physics does not just tell me about bridges. It tells me about the principles from which bridge behaviour follows as a special case. On the other hand, knowing about steel (e.g. its tensile strength) does not give me much knowledge about timber.

# Knowledge-seeking maximisation: initial definition
Is not about the quantity of facts held, but about compression ratio, i.e. about how much of reality a given piece of knowledge unlocks, predicts and/or makes intelligible. The best knowledge does the most work.

# Knowledge-seeking maximisation: ordering principle
That suggests an ordering principle:

> Seek knowledge that is generative of other knowledge first.

But what sits at the top of that hierarchy?

Several candidates present themselves:

- Logic and mathematics
  - The structure of valid inference itself
  - Without this:
    - Reasoning about anything is unreliable
    - Failures propagate everywhere (due to no self-correction)
- The nature of mind and perception
  - All knowledge arrives through my apparatus of knowing
  - Knowing its nature and limits precedes understanding its data
- Causality and systems thinking:
  - How things produce other things
  - This is so surface effects are traced to underlying structure
- The nature of models themselves
  - What it means for a representation to correspond to reality
  - Where models break down

**NOTE**:

- This looks like wisdom rather than mere knowledge
- It is self-referential: <br> *Effective knowledge-seeking needs a prior model of knowledge's structured* <br> I.e.: **I cannot fully bootstrap from nothing**

> - I start somewhere, i.e. with some prior model
> - The path doubles back, revising earlier assumptions
> 
> This implies:
> 
> - It is not a ladder with a fixed bottom rung
> - It is more like a web that I keep tightening/reinforcing

# Knowledge-seeking maximisation: what to ignore
The inverse of the following principle (which was given above):

> Seek knowledge that is generative of other knowledge first.

Hence, the above becomes:

> Ignore knowledge that not generalise.
> 
> => Ignore:
> 
> - Facts isolated from broader structure
> - Knowledge that is irrelevant to my trajectory through reality

But there is a tension here: a priori, me often cannot always know what will generalise. Faraday's work with magnets looked like trivia until Maxwell arrived. Hence, there is a meta-principle involved here that is crucial for effective knowledge-seeking in the long-term:

> - Maintain enough breadth to catch unexpected connections
> - While prioritising depth along axes of high leverage

# Knowledge-seeking maximisation: final definition
Maximising contact with reality means increasing the proportion of reality that is intelligible to me, weighted by the structural importance of what becomes intelligible. ***This implies not just seeing more, but seeing more of what matters for seeing everything else.*** In other words, it is like charting a map where me prioritise the areas that, once charted, let me infer the most about uncharted territory.
