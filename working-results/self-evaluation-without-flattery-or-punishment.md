# Statement
Self-evaluation is the reflexive application of [worth-applied-to-the-self.md](./worth-applied-to-the-self.md), [felt-states-as-data-and-register.md](./felt-states-as-data-and-register.md), and [internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md](./internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md) to oneself, not a new standard. It has two failure directions, both correspondence failures rather than independent errors: **self-flattery**, which credits a worth-standing the self's systems do not actually support, and **self-punishment**, which treats a within-system failure, an integration failure, or a mere capacity limit as a verdict on the whole self, or as evidence of a failure of rational commitment specifically.

# Derivation
> **NOTE**: This entry does not derive a new standard for judging oneself - it applies machinery already established, reflexively, to the self doing the evaluating. Worth already supplies the standard; [felt-states-as-data-and-register.md](./felt-states-as-data-and-register.md) already supplies the diagnostic for reading a signal correctly; [internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine](./internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md) already supplies the trigger condition for when neglect becomes fault. What follows states this explicitly and names the two failure directions using that machinery, rather than re-deriving a fresh chain of reasoning for each.

**Self-flattery** is over-crediting, i.e. claiming a worth-standing (per [worth-applied-to-the-self.md](./worth-applied-to-the-self.md)) that the actual state of one's systems and their integration does not support. **Self-punishment** is not simply the symmetric case of under-crediting - it specifically mistakes the part for the whole, or a given for a choice. [the-self-as-a-system-of-systems.md](./the-self-as-a-system-of-systems.md) already distinguishes a failure within a given system from a failure of integration between systems that are each individually sound; self-punishment is what happens when either of these local failures is read as a global verdict on the self as such, rather than as the specific, locatable failure it actually is.

**"Honour" (source essay 1) supplies the check against chasing an external substitute for the actual standard.** The source essay draws self-esteem (an internal matter - "regarding oneself as virtuous and aligned in thought and action with one's own values") apart from honour (its interpersonal reflection), and is explicit that these can diverge in either direction: *"the absence of this visibility and validation does not imply the absence of virtue, and conversely, the presence of the former does not imply the presence of the latter."* Applied here: self-evaluation must track worth's actual standing (the internal matter), not external validation or its absence. Chasing the latter as if it were the former is a form of self-flattery (borrowed credit) when validation is present without real standing, and a form of self-punishment (unearned deficit) when standing is real but validation is withheld or absent.

**"Threats to Conviction" (from source essay 2) supplies a reusable diagnostic for self-flattery specifically.** The source essay observes that a conviction can only feel "threatened" if challenging it seems likely to diminish one's efficacy - and that this fear itself "betrays an evasiveness at the root," since a conviction genuinely drawn from a rational grasp of reality cannot be threatened by scrutiny. Applied reflexively: a self-evaluation that cannot tolerate being tested - that flinches from an honest check of whether the credited worth-standing actually holds - is, by that same signature, showing itself to be inflated. This is the same defending-versus-testing signature [internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md](./internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md) already uses to detect evasion, applied here specifically to detect self-flattery rather than avoidance of an external premise.

**"Rationality vs. Intelligence" (source essay 3) supplies the check against self-punishment for a mere capacity limit.** The source essay draws a sharp line: *"intelligence is a capacity. Rationality, however, is an epistemological commitment... intelligence and rationality are two separate attributes, not unrelated, but not tightly coupled."* Capacity is a given, not a choice; commitment is chosen. Self-punishment frequently collapses this distinction, treating a capacity limit (a given) as though it were evidence of a failure of commitment (a choice). This is the same category error [the-subconscious-as-apparatus.md](./the-subconscious-as-apparatus.md) already forecloses at the system level - holding oneself responsible, in the sense proper to chosen rational commitment, for something that was never a matter of choice to begin with.

**"On Being Intelligent" (source essay 4) supplies the check against fixating on an image of worth rather than worth itself.** The source essay treats "being intelligent" as, at best, a welcome consequence of purposeful engagement with reality, never a valid goal in itself - fixating on the appearance of a value "detached from these primary pursuits" is "potentially deleterious" to the pursuit itself, exactly the same error-shape already named and set aside for happiness. Self-evaluation aimed at an image of one's own worth, rather than at worth's actual standing, is this same error turned inward.

# Domain of validity
Scoped as the rest of this section is scoped - a volitional, conceptual consciousness capable of the kind of self-directed causation established in [action-as-total-expression.md](./action-as-total-expression.md), applying [worth-applied-to-the-self.md](./worth-applied-to-the-self.md)'s standard reflexively to itself. As with [internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md](./internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md), this is scoped to the cognitive/psychological system and to the chosen commitment of rationality specifically, not to capacity or to biological standing.

**Checked against** [the-subconscious-as-apparatus.md](./the-subconscious-as-apparatus.md)'s system-scoping: Does treating a capacity limit as exempt from self-punishment risk excusing everything as "mere capacity," including things that are actually failures of commitment? It should not: the rationality-versus-intelligence distinction from source essay 3 cuts both ways - a capacity limit is exempt, but the choice to exercise or neglect one's rational commitment given whatever capacity one has is not, and remains fully subject to [internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md](./internalised-premises-and-the-responsibility-to-examine.md)'s account of genuine occasion.

**Checked against overcorrection**: Does drawing self-esteem apart from honour risk implying social validation or feedback is worthless? It should not be read this way - honour is named in the source essay as a genuine by-product and can carry real evidentiary value (per [felt-states-as-data-and-register.md](./felt-states-as-data-and-register.md)'s evidentiary role), but it is not the standard itself, and should not be substituted for it in either direction.

# Open edges
- **The cross-component weighting problem is inherited, not resolved.** [worth-applied-to-the-self.md](./worth-applied-to-the-self.md) left open how to weigh a gain in one worth-component against a loss in another. Self-evaluation will need this in any real, mixed case and does not resolve it here.
- **The capacity/commitment line is not always sharp.** Cases like persistent procrastination sit ambiguously between "a capacity limit" and "a neglected commitment," and this entry does not supply a way to tell them apart beyond restating that the distinction matters.
- **The "cannot tolerate challenge" diagnostic may have confounds.** Declining to re-litigate a bad-faith or exhausting challenge is not automatically evidence of an inflated self-evaluation; this entry has not distinguished that case from genuine evasiveness.
